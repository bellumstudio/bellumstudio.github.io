# Crumb Scriptwriter Trial Assignment

Welcome, and thanks for your interest in writing for Crumb. This trial is designed to evaluate your fit as a scriptwriter for our channel.

Before you begin, we strongly recommend watching several of our most recent or most popular videos to get a feel for our tone and style: [youtube.com/@Crumb](https://www.youtube.com/@Crumb)

---

## The Assignment

**Find a recent story** that fits Crumb's core theme: **cyber-crime, heists, or fraud.** The story should be compelling, have interesting characters, and enough depth to sustain a 20+ minute video. Then produce the two deliverables described below.

---

## Deliverable 1: Story Outline

Write a short outline for your chosen story. It should include:

- **The story you've chosen** and a brief explanation of why it's a strong fit for the channel (1–2 sentences).
- **A 3-act structure overview** breaking the story into a clear beginning, middle, and end. For each act, write 2–4 sentences summarizing the key beats, emotional arc, and narrative tension. Think of it like a mini movie. We want to see rising action, a climax, and a resolution.
- **A working title** or tagline suggestion that would hook a viewer's attention on YouTube.
- **A source list** at the bottom of the outline. This should include every source you consulted during research: articles, court documents, forum threads, podcasts, YouTube videos, Wayback Machine snapshots, books, everything. The more sources, the better. We use these during the edit and they demonstrate the depth of your research.

### What We're Looking For in the Outline

1. **Research depth.** We don't want a summary of the top three Google results. We want to see that you dug into the story. That means court filings, archived pages on the Wayback Machine, claims cross-referenced across multiple sources, podcasts or interviews, and angles that aren't widely covered. Your source list should reflect this.
2. **Story instinct.** Can you identify the meatiest, most dramatic parts of a story and structure them into a narrative that builds and pays off? We're making mini movies, not Wikipedia summaries.

---

## Deliverable 2: Test Write (3 Paragraphs, Starting From the Hook)

Write the **first 3 paragraphs** of the script, beginning with the hook.

The hook is the most important part of any Crumb video. Its job is to grab the viewer in the first 30–40 seconds and make them need to keep watching. This could mean jumping straight into the most dramatic moment of the story, asking questions that will be answered later, or giving a rapid-fire preview of the craziest details to come. Whatever approach you take, it should create immediate intrigue and signal that the viewer is in for something worth their time.

After the hook, continue into the opening of the story, setting up the world, introducing the key character(s), and beginning the chronological narrative.

### Writing Guidelines

- **Write for a voiceover, not a blog post.** This script will be read aloud, so it needs to flow naturally when spoken. Read your paragraphs out loud to check. Avoid overly complex language. The goal is for any viewer to follow along easily without pausing.
- **Narrator voice.** Default to telling the story in third person, letting events unfold rather than commenting on them like a news anchor. That said, occasional first-person interjections can land really well, especially for psychological beats or getting inside a character's head. Lines like *"and I think, for Joel, he felt invincible at this point"* are the kind of thing viewers eat up, because they offer real insight rather than just opinion. Use them sparingly, and only when they add something a pure third-person line can't. Either way, speak in factual statements, but tell them in an engaging, story-like way.
- **Show, don't report.** We want to feel like we're there. Use vivid, specific details. Create tension. Build toward something. This is not a news article. It's the opening of a mini movie.
- **Keep it PG.** If the story involves sensitive content, use careful wording that won't risk YouTube demonetization (e.g., "adult content" instead of explicit terms).
- **No filler.** Every sentence should move the story forward. If something isn't interesting or necessary, cut it.
- **Don't speak in absolutes** unless factually verified. Prefer "the majority of people" over "everyone" and "virtually unknown" over "nobody knew."

### What We're Looking For in the Test Write

1. **Storytelling ability.** Can you write a hook that genuinely makes someone want to keep watching? Does your writing have momentum, emotion, and a sense of drama, without manufacturing it?

2. **Video references with timestamps AND transcribed quotes.** This is critical. Wherever your narrative references a moment from a news clip, interview, podcast, documentary, or any other video source, **include a direct timestamped link AND write out what is being said in that clip directly below the link.** It should look like this:

   > Martinez's arrest was captured live on the local news, the officers swarming the parking lot as bewildered casino patrons looked on.
   >
   > **[PLAY VIDEO: Local CBS affiliate coverage of arrest, https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=EXAMPLE from 2:14 to 2:47]**
   >
   > *"We have a suspect in custody. Authorities are calling this one of the largest casino fraud cases in state history, and the man you see being walked out is believed to be at the center of it."*

   This technique is how we bring other "characters" into the video. Real footage, real voices, instead of having the entire story re-narrated by a single voiceover. Think of it as directing, not just writing. Use videos to *drive* the narrative rather than telling everything like a report.

   **Writing out the spoken quote underneath each video link is non-negotiable.** Without it, every link has to be opened and watched during script editing just to figure out what's actually being said, which slows the entire production down. The transcribed quote also lets us swap in or trim clips later without losing the context of what the moment was supposed to convey.

3. **Sourcing for claims and facts.** Any specific claim, a dollar amount, a date, a legal outcome, a quote, should have a source. You can do this inline using hyperlinked text, or by using comments/footnotes that reference your source list. The key is that we can verify every factual statement you make. If only one source supports a claim, flag it explicitly (e.g., "According to reporting by *Wired*..."). We take factual accuracy extremely seriously. There is a legal concept called "duty of care," and when discussing someone's alleged crimes, being factual isn't optional.

## Formatting & Submission

- Submit both deliverables in a single **Google Doc**. Set sharing to "anyone with the link can view."
- Format cleanly with proper spacing, paragraph breaks, and section headings (Outline / Test Write).
- The source list should be clearly organized as comments on the document, or at the bottom of the document. Include URLs, publication names, and dates where possible.
- Video references should be clearly marked in the body of the text (as shown in the example above), not buried in the source list.

## A Few Final Notes

- **Take your time.** There is no deadline. Quality matters more than speed.
- **Be original.** We can tell when something is fully written by AI. You can use AI, but if it feels or reads like AI, we will have to take that into consideration.
- **Do a story that genuinely interests you.** Pick a story that genuinely interests you. That passion will come through in the writing. If you have multiple story ideas, feel free to pitch them and we can discuss which one to pursue.
